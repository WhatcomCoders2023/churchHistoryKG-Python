<h1>The Ecumenical Movement Unites Denominations</h1>
<ul>
<li><strong>Period:</strong> !!smallcaps|ad!! <a href="date.1908-2022">1908–Present</a></li>
<li><strong>Description:</strong> The ecumenical movement led to renewed relationships within the universal church.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Summary</h2>
<p>Ecumenism, a movement that promoted unity and cooperation within the universal church, developed in part out of the foreign missionary movement of the nineteenth century. While Christianity has a long history of denominations dividing over doctrine and practice, from the Great Schism between East and West to the diversifying of Protestantism, the beginning of the twentieth century saw the universal church unite around a common goal of evangelism. The ecumenical movement emphasized Christian cooperation across not only denominational lines, but geographical boundaries, as churches across the globe collaborated to form the World Council of Churches. The Roman Catholic Church, in the spirit of ecumenism, appealed to unity and closer relations with the Orthodox and Protestant churches. While maintaining doctrinal distinctives, the universal church affirmed its common central belief in Jesus Christ.</p>
<h2>Key Developments</h2>
<!--- A bulleted list of important developments during that period.-->

<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ecumenism">Ecumenism</a> developed among Protestants around the shared goal of evangelism (<a href="date.1908-2022">1908–2022</a>).</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Denominations">Denominations</a> gathered at conferences to discuss <a href="bk.%evangelism">evangelism</a> and the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.ChurchUnity">unity of the church</a> (<a href="date.1910-2010">1910–2010</a>).</li>
<li>The <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25WorldCouncilofChurches">World Council of Churches</a> was established to unify churches across denominations and geographical boundaries (<a href="date.1948">1948</a>).</li>
<li>Some Protestant denominations merged or reunited to establish "United" denominations (<a href="date.1900+100+m">mid-1900s</a>).</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.tle%3asecondvaticancouncil">Vatican II</a> advocated for unity among Christians and acknowledged the dignity of other faiths outside of Christianity (<a href="date.1962-1965">1962–1965</a>).</li>
<li>The unity of the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25RomanCatholicism_Belief">Roman Catholic</a> and the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25EasternOrthodoxy_Belief">Eastern Orthodox</a> and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25OrientalOrthodoxChurches">Oriental Orthodox</a> churches was reaffirmed (<a href="date.1995">1995</a>).</li>
</ul>

<h2>Key Concepts</h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25EcumenicalMovement">Ecumenical movement</a> arose in the <a href="date.1901+100">twentieth century</a> and sought to foster Christian unity across <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Denominations">denominations</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ecumenism">Ecumenism</a> encouraged Christians to work together for the sake of the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Gospel">gospel</a>.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%evangelism">Evangelism</a> was a top priority of the ecumenical movement that united churches.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25FaithAndOrder">Faith and Order</a> was part of the early ecumenical movement; organized ecumenical conferences in Lausanne and Edinburgh; now a part of the World Council of Churches.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25LifeandWork">Life and Work</a> was part of the ecumenical movement that focused on social issues; organized ecumenical conferences in Stockholm and Edinburgh.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%LausanneMovement">Lausanne Movement</a> was established in 1976 to promote worldwide evangelism and ecumenism among denominations, also known as the Lausanne Committee for World Evangelization; was founded after the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.tle%3afirstinternationalcongress1">International Congress on World Evangelization</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25MalinesConversations">Malines Conversations</a> explored the potential reunion of the Roman Catholic Church and the Church of England.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%WeekPrayerChristianUnity">Week of Prayer for Christian Unity</a> is an ecumenical observance that is organized by the World Council of Churches; it began as the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ChurchUnityOctave">Octave of Christian Unity</a> within the Roman Catholic Church.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25WorldCouncilofChurches">World Council of Churches</a> was established in 1948 as an ecumenical organization of churches including <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Protestantism_Belief">Protestants</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Anglicanism_Belief">Anglicans</a>, and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25EasternOrthodoxy_Belief">Eastern Orthodox</a> churches; the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25RomanCatholicism_Belief">Roman Catholic Church</a> is a participant but not a full member.</li>
</ul>
