<h1>New Orthodoxy Develops in Response to Protestant Liberalism</h1>
<ul>
<li><strong>Period:</strong> !!smallcaps|ad!! <a href="date.1914-1945">1914–1945</a></li>
<li><strong>Description:</strong> In the aftermath of World War I, a dialectical theology developed in response to what Neo-Orthodox scholars saw as the failures of Protestant liberalism's optimistic view of humanity.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Summary</h2>
<p>Like Protestant liberalism, the Neo-Orthodox movement, also known as dialectical theology or crisis theology, was a diverse theological movement that developed in Germany. The moral crises of the early twentieth century, from World War I to the rise of Nazi Germany, led some theologians to challenge the ideas of Protestant liberalism. While Neo-Orthodox theologians were typically united in their opposition to the theology of liberalism and its optimistic views of human nature and the progress of human history, disagreements were frequent and often led to splits among its adherents. The movement flourished in Europe throughout the 1930s and 1940s, but eventually declined.</p>
<h2>Key Developments</h2>
<!--- A bulleted list of important developments during that period.-->

<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25nationalism">Nationalism</a> led to a rise in <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Totalitarianism">totalitarian</a> regimes and a greater emphasis on <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lco.National_pride">national identities</a> (<a href="date.1800+100+l-1900+100+e">late 1800s–early 1900s</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.tle%3aworldwari">World War I</a> challenged <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Liberalism_Belief">liberalism's</a> optimism regarding <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.HumanNature">human nature</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25sin">sin</a>, and the progress of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25history">history</a> (<a href="date.1914-1918">1914–1918</a>).</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Neoorthodoxy_Belief">Neo-Orthodoxy</a> developed in response to perceived inadequacies of <a href="bk.%ProtestantLiberalism">Protestant liberalism</a> and the aftermath of World War I (<a href="date.1900+100+e">early 1900s</a>).</li>
<li>The <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25dialectic">dialectical method</a>, which set two seemingly contradictory ideas in comparison, was applied to the study of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25theology">theology</a> (<a href="date.1900+100+e">early 1900s</a>).</li>
<li>The <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.GodsInfinity">transcendence of God</a> was emphasized over attempts to explain or define God in human terms (<a href="date.1900+100+e">early 1900s</a>).</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.SpecialRevelation">Revelation</a> was emphasized over <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25NaturalTheology_Belief">natural theology</a> for gaining knowledge of God; knowledge of God was impossible without divine revelation, most notably the revelation in the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.Incarnation">incarnation of Jesus</a> (<a href="date.1900+100+e">early 1900s</a>).</li>
<li>The <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25JesusChrist_Deity">Jesus</a> of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25faith">faith</a> was emphasized over the historical Jesus (<a href="date.1900+100+e">early 1900s</a>).</li>
<li>Some theologians advocated for the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25demythologization">demythologization</a> of the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25NewTestament_Writing">New Testament</a> in order to communicate the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25kerygma">kerygma</a> of the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Gospels_Writing">Gospels</a> to a modern world (<a href="date.1940+10">1940s</a>).</li>
</ul>

<h3><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Neoorthodoxy_Belief">Neo-Orthodox Theologians</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25RudolfBultmann_Person">Rudolf Bultmann</a> (<a href="date.1884.8.20-1976.7.30">August 20, 1884–July 30, 1976</a>): German Lutheran <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Neoorthodoxy_Belief">Neo-Orthodox</a> <a href="bk.%theologian">theologian</a> and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25professor">professor</a>; he studied at the universities of <a href="bk.%UniversityofMarburg">Marburg</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25UniversityOfTubingen_Place">Tübingen</a>, and <a href="bk.%UniversityOfBerlin_Place">Berlin</a>, and taught at the University of Marburg; he viewed the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25NewTestament_Writing">New Testament</a> as a <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25myth">myth</a> which needs to be <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25demythologization">demythologized</a>; author of <a href="bk.%HistoryOfTheSynopticTradition_Writing"><em>The History of the Synoptic Tradition</em></a> and <a href="bk.%NewTestamentAndMythology_Writing"><em>New Testament and Mythology</em></a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25KarlBarth_Person">Karl Barth</a> (<a href="date.1886.5.10-1968.12.10">May 10, 1886–December 10, 1968</a>): Swiss <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ReformedGroup_Denom">Reformed</a> theologian; founder of the Neo-Orthodox movement; he studied at the universities of Berlin, Tübingen, and Marburg, and taught at the universities of <a href="bk.%UniversityOfGottingen_Place">Göttingen</a> and <a href="bk.%UniversityOfMunster_Place">Münster</a>; opposed Protestant liberalism; author of <a href="bk.%DerRomerbrief_Writing"><em>The Epistle to the Romans</em></a>, which developed Barth's dialectical theology, and <a href="bk.%ChurchDogmatics_Writing"><em>Church Dogmatics</em></a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25FriedrichGogarten_Person">Friedrich Gogarten</a> (<a href="date.1887.1.13-1967.10.16">January 13, 1887–October 16, 1967</a>): German Lutheran Neo-Orthodox theologian; he taught at the universities of <a href="bk.%UniversityOfJena_Place">Jena</a> and Göttingen; influenced by Martin Luther and his views on <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25solaFide">Sola Fide</a>; author of <a href="bk.%DemythologizationAndHistory_Writing"><em>Demythologization and History</em></a>, which builds upon Bultmann's work.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25PaulTillich_Person">Paul Tillich</a> (<a href="date.1896-1965">1896–1965</a>): German-American Protestant Neo-Orthodox theologian; adherent of Christian existentialism; he studied at the universities of Berlin, Tübingen, and <a href="bk.@UniversityOfHalle">Halle</a>, and taught at the universities of Marburg, <a href="bk.%UniversityOfFrankfurt_Place">Frankfurt</a>, <a href="bk.%UnionTheologicalSeminary">Union Theological Seminary</a>, <a href="bk.%HarvardDivinitySeminary_Place">Harvard Divinity School</a>, and the <a href="bk.%UniversityOfChicago_Place">University of Chicago</a>; author of <a href="bk.%TillichSystematicTheology_Writing"><em>Systematic Theology</em></a>, which reflects his adherence to <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25existentialism">Christian existentialism</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25EmilBrunner_Person">Emil Brunner</a> (<a href="date.1889.12.23-1966.4.6">December 23, 1889–April 6, 1966</a>): Swiss Reformed Neo-Orthodox theologian; he taught at the <a href="bk.%UniversityOfZurich_Place">University of Zurich</a>; influenced by <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.MartinLuther">Martin Luther</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.JohnCalvin">John Calvin</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ImmanuelKant_Person">Immanuel Kant</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25SorenAabyeKierkegaard_Person">Søren Kierkegaard</a>, and Karl Barth, though he disagreed with Barth over the usefulness of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25NaturalTheology_Belief">natural theology</a>, which he addressed in <a href="bk.%NatureAndGrace_Writing"><em>Nature and Grace</em></a>; opposed Protestant liberalism. </li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ReinholdNiebuhr_Person">Reinhold Niebuhr</a> (<a href="date.1892.6.21-1971.6.1">June 21, 1892–June 1, 1971</a>): American Neo-Orthodox theologian and pastor; taught at Union Theological Seminary; he opposed Protestant liberalism and was influenced by Karl Barth, Emil Brunner, and Søren Kierkegaard; developed <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Realism_Belief">Christian realism</a> and opposed pacifism, urging Christians not to remain neutral during wartime; author of <a href="bk.%MoralManAndImmoralSociety_Writing"><em>Moral Man and Immoral Society</em></a>, which argues that individual morality influences group morality.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.DietrichBonhoeffer">Dietrich Bonhoeffer</a> (<a href="date.1906.2.4-1945.4.9">February 4, 1906–April 9, 1945</a>): German Lutheran Neo-Orthodox theologian and pastor; he was influenced by Barth; author of <a href="bk.%TheCostOfDiscipleship_Writing"><em>The Cost of Discipleship</em></a>, which emphasizes "costly grace" over what he perceived as the "cheap grace" of liberalism.</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25philosopher">Philosophers</a></h3>

<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25SorenAabyeKierkegaard_Person">Søren Aabye Kierkegaard</a> (<a href="date.1813.5.5-1855.11.11">May 5, 1813–November 11, 1855</a>): Danish <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25philosopher">philosopher</a> and theologian; he developed Christian existentialism and was influential to Neo-Orthodox theologians. </li>

<h2>Key Concepts</h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="bk.%Realism_Belief">Christian realism</a>, a way of thinking which emphasized the tension between the reality of sin and the hope of redemption, was developed by Reinhold Niebuhr.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25existentialism">Christian existentialism</a> was influential to some Neo-Orthodox theologians, including Rudolf Bultmann.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25demythologization">Demythologization</a> was developed by Rudolf Bultmann.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25dialectic">Dialectic</a> methodology was applied to the study of theology.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Neoorthodoxy_Belief">Neo-Orthodoxy</a>, a movement also known as dialectical theology or theology of crisis, was developed by Karl Barth, Emil Brunner, and Friedrich Gogarten; it developed in response to <a href="bk.%ProtestantLiberalism">Protestant liberalism</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25transcendence">Transcendence</a> and the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25sovereignty">sovereignty</a> of God, as well as the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25sin">sinfulness</a> of humanity, were emphasized as Neo-Orthodoxy developed.</li>
</ul>

<h3><a href="bk.%commentary.explication">Commentaries</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="bk.%DerRomerbrief_Writing"><em>Epistle to the Romans (Der Römerbrief)</em></a> (Karl Barth, <a href="date.1919">1919</a>): addresses the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.GodsSovereignty">sovereignty of God</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.pt%3aGod_Grace">grace</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.SpecialRevelation">revelation</a>, and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25sin">sin</a>; rejects <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25liberalTheology">liberal theology</a>; led to the spread of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Neoorthodoxy_Belief">dialectical theology</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="bk.%essay">Essays</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="bk.%NewTestamentAndMythology_Writing"><em>New Testament and Mythology</em></a> (Rudolf Bultmann, <a href="date.1941">1941</a>): advocates for demythologizing the New Testament in order to communicate its message to the modern world.</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="bk.%systematicTheology">Systematic Theologies</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="bk.%ChurchDogmatics_Writing"><em>Church Dogmatics</em></a> (Karl Barth, <a href="date.1932-1967">1932–1967</a>): systematic theology published in fourteen volumes; addresses <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.ScriptureAndRevelation">the doctrine of Scripture and revelation</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.Trinity">the doctrine of the Triune God</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.Creation">creation</a>, and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.Salvation">the Holy Spirit and salvation</a>.</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="bk.%treatise">Treatise</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="bk.%HistoryOfTheSynopticTradition_Writing"><em>The History of the Synoptic Tradition</em></a> (Rudolf Bultmann, <a href="date.1921">1921</a>): applies <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25formCriticism">form criticism</a> to the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25SynopticGospels_Writing">Synoptic Gospels</a>; articulates Bultmann's view that the New Testament, and especially the Gospels, are not historical accounts of the life of Jesus but rather are reflective of the early church's theology.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%MoralManAndImmoralSociety_Writing"><em>Moral Man and Immoral Society</em></a> (Reinhold Niebuhr, <a href="date.1932">1932</a>): addresses <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.PersonalSin">personal</a> and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.StructuralSin">structural human sin</a> with an emphasis on the communal nature of sin; argues that individual morality influences group morality; rejects the optimistic humanism of liberal theology.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%TheCostOfDiscipleship_Writing"><em>The Cost of Discipleship</em></a> (Dietrich Bonhoeffer, <a href="date.1937">1937</a>): addresses the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bible.63.6.20-63.6.49">Sermon on the Mount</a>, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.pt%3aDiscipleship">discipleship</a>, and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.pt%3aGod_Grace">God’s grace</a>; Bonhoeffer advocates for "costly grace" over what he perceived to be the "cheap grace" of liberalism.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%TheNatureAndDestinyOfMan_Writing"><em>The Nature and Destiny of Man</em></a> (Reinhold Niebuhr, <a href="date.1941">1941</a>): articulates a Neo-Orthodox view of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lsto.HumanNature">human nature</a>.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%DemythologizationAndHistory_Writing"><em>Demythologization and History</em></a> (Friedrich Gogarten, <a href="date.1955">1955</a>): builds on the work of Rudolf Bultmann and existentialism. </li>
</ul>
