<h1>The Chinese Church Grows Despite Persecution</h1>
<ul>
<li><strong>Period:</strong> !!smallcaps|ad!! 1949–Present</li>
<li><strong>Description:</strong> Despite years of violent oppression during the Cultural Revolution, the church in China grew rapidly through an underground movement. The Chinese church continued to expand  </li>
</ul>
<h2>Key Developments</h2>
<!--- A bulleted list of important developments during that period.-->

<ul>
<li>The People's Republic of China was established by the Chinese Communist Party.</li>
<li>Under an atheist and anti-imperialist government, foreign missionaries were expelled.</li>
<li>The Chinese Communist Party established five national religious organizations, including the Protestant Three-Self Patriotic Movement, into which all Protestant denominations were merged, and the Chinese Patriotic Catholic Association.</li>
<li>Mao initiated the Cultural Revolution to rid Chinese society of any opposition to Chinese communism.</li>
<li>Churches and other places of worship were closed that contributed to foreign influence in China, including the Three-Self Patriotic Movement and the Chinese Patriotic Catholic Association.</li>
<li>Opponents were persecuted, including Christians; some were imprisoned.</li>
<li>The church in China moved underground, meeting in homes, and began to expand rapidly.</li>
<li>The Cultural Revolution came to an end following the death of Mao though resistance to religion still remained.</li>
<li>China began to open up to the world under new Communist leadership.</li>
<li>Churches were allowed to reopen.</li>
</ul>

<h3><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25RomanCatholicism_Belief">Roman Catholics</a></h3>
<ul>
<li>Ignatius Kung Pin-Mei (August 2, 1901–March 12, 2000): Roman Catholic <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25bishop">Bishop</a> of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%40Shanghai">Shanghai</a>; opposed the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.tle%3aculturalrevolutionchina">Cultural Revolution in China</a> and was imprisoned for 30 years; made <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25cardinal">cardinal</a> by <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.PopeJohnPaulII">John Paul II</a> in 1979.</li>
<li>Beda Chang (<em>c.</em> 1905–November 11, 1951): Chinese <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Jesuits_People">Jesuit</a> <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%23priest">priest</a>; he was <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%23martyr">martyred</a> under the communist government of the People's Republic of China.[^1]</li>
</ul>
<h3>Political Leaders</h3>
<ul>
<li>Mao Zedong (December 26, 1893–September 9, 1976): Chinese <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Communism_Belief">communist</a> revolutionary; leader of the Chinese Communist Party; founder of the People's Republic of China; developed Maoism ideology influenced by Marxism-Leninism; author of the May 16 Notification which started the Cultural Revolution.</li>
<li>Deng Xiaoping (September 13, 1982–November 2, 1987): Chinese revolutionary; succeeded Mao as leader of the People's Republic of China; introduced reforms in <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%40China">China</a> and relaxed governmental control over religion.</li>
<li>Gang of Four (20th century) was a group of four officials from the Communist Party of China who were leaders of the Cultural Revolution; they were sentenced to life in prison for crimes committed during the Cultural Revolution.</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Protestantism_Belief">Protestants</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25YTWu_Person">Y. T. Wu</a>: (November 4, 1893–September 17, 1979): Chinese <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Protestantism_Belief">Protestant</a> leader; founded the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ThreeSelfPatrioticMovement">Three-Self Patriotic Movement</a>. </li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25TzuchenChao_Person">Tzu-ch’en Chao</a> (1888-1979): Chinese Protestant leader and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25theologian">theologian</a>; supporter of the Three-Self Patriotic Movement; signed <em>The Christian Manifesto</em>. </li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25WatchmanNee_Person">Watchman Nee</a> (November 4, 1903–May 30, 1972): Chinese <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25indigenousChurch">indigenous church</a> leader and founder of the Local Church movement; he was arrested under communist rule and died as a prisoner.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25KHTing_Person">K. H. Ting</a> (September 20, 1915–November 22, 2012): <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Anglicanism_Belief">Anglican</a> Bishop of Zhejiang; Chinese statesman; Chairperson emeritus of the Three-Self Patriotic Movement; President emeritus of the China Christian Council.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Key Concepts</h2>
<ul>
<li>Chinese Christian Council was established by the Protestant churches in China.</li>
<li>Chinese Patriotic Catholic Association is part of the state-sanctioned Chinese Church; not recognized by the Vatican. </li>
<li>Chung Hua Sheng Kung Hui was the Anglican Church in China which was merged into the Three-Self Patriotic Movement.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Communism_Belief">Communism</a> attempted to suppress Christianity in China.</li>
<li>Maoism was developed by Mao Zedong as a type of communism.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ThreeSelfPatrioticMovement">Three-Self Patriotic Movement</a> is part of the state-sanctioned Chinese Church; advocates for self-governance, self-support, and self-propagation; originally named the Three-Self Reform Movement.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Encyclicals</h3>
<ul>
<li><em>Cupimus imprimis</em> (<a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25PopePiusXII_Person">Pope Pius XII</a>, 1952): papal encyclical written during the persecution of Chinese Christians in the People's Republic of China; offers encouragement to Chinese Catholics who remained faithful amidst persecution.</li>
<li><em>Ad Sinarum gentem</em> (Pope Pius XII, 1954); papal encyclical written during the persecution of Chinese Christians in the People's Republic of China; offers encouragement to Chinese Catholics who remained faithful amidst persecution.</li>
<li><em>Ad Apostolorum principis</em> (Pope Pius XII, 1958): papal encyclical written during the persecution of Chinese Christians in the People' Republic of China; criticizes the Chinese Patriotic Catholic Association and offers encouragement to Chinese Catholics amidst persecution.</li>
</ul>
<h3>Governing Documents</h3>
<ul>
<li>Constitution of the People's Republic of China (1982): grants citizens freedom of religion; includes clause  "the State protects normal religious activities" (Article 36).[^2]</li>
</ul>
<h3>Manifestos</h3>
<ul>
<li><em>The Christian Manifesto</em> (1950): written by Protestants in China; supported the People's Republic of China and the Communist Party of China.</li>
<li>May 16 Notification (Mao Zedong. 1966): circular issued during a meeting of the Chinese Communist Party in Beijing which launched the Cultural Revolution.</li>
</ul>
