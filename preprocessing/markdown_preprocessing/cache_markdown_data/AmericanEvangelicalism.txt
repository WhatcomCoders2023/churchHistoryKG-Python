<h1>American Evangelicalism Diverges As it Responds to Modernism</h1>
<ul>
<li><strong>Period:</strong> !!smallcaps|ad!! <a href="date.1859-2022">1859-present</a></li>
<li><strong>Description:</strong> American evangelicalism responded to modernism with varied approaches to the changing culture. Evangelicalism rose to prominence in American society and politics in the second half of the twentieth century.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Summary</h2>
<p>Evangelicalism, which emphasizes personal conversion (or spiritual rebirth) and evangelism, is a diverse movement within the United States and has played an influential role in shaping contemporary American culture and politics. At the turn of the twentieth century, Protestant evangelicals were deeply divided over how the church should approach the challenges facing modern society, as well as how to respond to Protestant liberalism. Advances in science and modern society brought changes to American culture and challenges to traditional authority and the objectivity of truth. While some in evangelicals attempted to thwart any modern influences on the faith, others embraced modernism in an attempt to reconcile new discoveries with Christianity in the hopes of affirming the relevancy of the faith to modern times. Neo-evangelicalism emerged among those who sought a middle way, a compromise between fundamentalism and modernism. This new evangelical movement was eager to engage the culture, unlike the fundamentalists, and remained affirming of traditional evangelical beliefs like biblical inerrancy, contrary to modernists. By the mid-twentieth century, neo-evangelicalism was on the rise largely due to the evangelical crusades of Billy Graham and establishment of evangelical organizations and universities. American evangelicalism continued to diversify with the emergence of the conservative evangelical right and progressive evangelical left. </p>

<h2>Key Concepts</h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="bk.%inerrancy">Biblical inerrancy</a> was a core belief of evangelicals; with the emergence of modernism and challenges to traditional views of Scripture, Protestants debated the inerrancy of Scripture.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25modernism_Belief">Christian modernism</a> was opposed to fundamentalism; attempted to reconcile Christianity with modern social and intellectual developments. </li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25conservativeEvangelicalism_Belief">Conservative evangelicalism</a> developed out of fundamentalism; affirms orthodox Christian doctrines and emphasizes the importance of biblical inerrancy. </li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Evangelicalism_Belief">Evangelicalism</a> developed as a diverse movement in Britain and the United States; emphasizes personal conversion (also referred to as being "born again"), the authority of the Bible, the atonement, and activism (namely, evangelism).[^1]</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Fundamentalism_Belief">Fundamentalism</a> was opposed to modernism; rejected Protestant liberalism and liberal interpretations of the Bible; affirmed biblical inerrancy and literal interpretations of the Bible.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%ProtestantLiberalism">Protestant liberalism</a> developed in <a href="date.1801+100">nineteenth century</a> Germany before spreading to the United States; challenged traditional views of Scripture and advocated for biblical criticism.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25postModernEvangelicalism">Postmodern evangelicalism</a> or progressive Christianity developed as a broad movement generally characterized by diversity, pluralism, and inclusivity; rejected conservative fundamentalism, liberal modernism, and mainstream neo-evangelicalism.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25rightWingConservatism">Right-wing conservatism</a> developed within evangelicalism and sought to reestablish traditional values within society.</li>
</ul>

<li><a href="bk.%ChristianityAndLiberalism_Writing"><em>Christianity and Liberalism</em></a> (John Gresham Machen; <a href="date.1923">1923</a>): addresses the shortcomings of liberalism and defends fundamentalism.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%FaithOfModernism_Writing"><em>The Faith of Modernism</em></a> (Shailer Mathews, <a href="date.1924">1924</a>): addresses the shortcomings of fundamentalism and defends theological modernism.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%UneasyConscienceModernFundamentalism_Writing"><em>The Uneasy Conscience of Modern Fundamentalism</em></a> (Carl F. H. Henry, <a href="date.1947">1947</a>): addresses the short-comings of fundamentalism in its application of Christianity to modern issues.</li>
<li><a href="bk.%FundamentalismAmericanCulture_Writing"><em>Fundamentalism and American Culture</em></a> (<a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.georgemarsden">George Mardsen</a>, <a href="date.1980">1980</a>): chronicles the history of Christian fundamentalism in North America; addresses dispensationalism, premillennialism, and the Holiness movement; it also addresses contemporary American fundamentalism and the rise of the religious right.</li>
</ul>
