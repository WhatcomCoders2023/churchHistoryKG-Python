<h1>The Rise of Puritanism</h1>
<ul>
<li><strong>Period:</strong> !!smallcaps|ad!! <a href="date.1553-1689">1553–1689</a></li>
<li><strong>Description:</strong> English dissenters sought to further reform the Church of England and purify society while separatists fled to America to establish the Puritan way of life. The push for freedom of worship helped to develop new Protestant denominations.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Key Developments</h2>
<!--- A bulleted list of important developments during that period.-->

<ul>
<li>Protestants were persecuted under Catholic Queen Mary Tudor and many were forced into exile.</li>
<li>Puritans and Anglicans disagreed over church government; presbyterianism was promoted by Puritans and the episcopate by Anglicans.</li>
<li>Elizabeth I established a compromise between Catholicism and Protestantism with the Elizabethan Settlement.</li>
<li>James I, and later Charles I, established themselves as absolute monarchs with the divine right to rule over the state and church. </li>
<li>Anglicans attempted to suppress Puritanism and make the English church uniform by requiring high-church practices.</li>
<li>Separatists left the Church of England and fled to the Netherlands.</li>
<li>Civil war broke out in England between Parliamentarians and Royalists.</li>
<li>Puritans initiated reforms to worship and church government.</li>
<li>Dissenters were suppressed as the Church of England attempted to establish uniformity.</li>
<li>Presbyterianism prevailed in Westminster Assembly while congregationalists argued that the true church was not confined to one place or form of government.</li>
<li>England became a Commonwealth.</li>
<li>The monarchy was reestablished following the death of Cromwell.</li>
<li>Anglicanism dominated the Church of England once again.</li>
<li>New denominations were established under the protection of religious tolerance in the American colonies.</li>
</ul>

<h3>Monarchs</h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25MaryTudor_Person">Mary Tudor</a> (<a href="date.1516.2.18-1558.11.17">February 18, 1516-November 17, 1558</a>): <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%23Queen">queen</a> of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%40England">England</a> and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%40Ireland">Ireland</a> (1553–1558); supported <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25RomanCatholicism_Belief">Roman Catholicism</a>; persecuted Protestants.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25QueenElizabethIEngland_Person">Elizabeth I</a> (<a href="date.1533.9.7-1603.3.24">September 7, 1533–March 24, 1603</a>): queen of England (1558--1603); supported Protestantism; issued Elizabethan Settlement that established a compromise between Roman Catholicism and Protestantism within the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ChurchOfEngland">Church of England</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25KingJamesIEngland_Person">James I</a> (<a href="date.1566.6.19-1625.3.27">June 19, 1566–March 27, 1625</a>): king of England, Ireland, and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%40Scotland">Scotland</a> (as James IV); he was Protestant but opposed Puritanism; <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.tle%3ajamesiholdsconferencehampton">convened the Hampton Court Conference</a>; supported the translation of the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25KingJamesVersion_Writing">King James Bible</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25KingCharlesIGreatBritain_Person">Charles I</a> (<a href="date.1600.11.19-1649.1.30">November 19, 1600–January 30, 1649</a>): king of England and Scotland; supporter of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Anglicanism_Belief]">Anglicanism</a>; he was opposed by Puritans which led to the English Civil War; executed in 1649 after losing the war.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25KingCharlesIIGreatBritain_Person">Charles II</a> (<a href="date.1630.5.29-1685.2.6">May 29, 1630–February 6, 1685</a>): king of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25GreatBritain_Place">Great Britain</a> and Ireland (1660--1685); supported <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Presbyterianism">Presbyterianism</a> to gain help from the Scots to reclaim the throne; returned to Anglicanism after he was defeated by Cromwell; reinstated as monarch following <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.tle%3adeatholivercromwell">Cromwell's death</a>.</li>
</ul>

<h3><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Puritans_People">Puritans</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25MatthewParker_Person">Matthew Parker</a> (<a href="date.1504.8.6-1575.5.17">August 6, 1504–May 17, 1575</a>): archbishop of Canterbury; helped develop the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ThirtyNineArticles_Writing"><em>Thirty-Nine Articles</em></a> and the Bishop's Bible.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ThomasCartwright_Person">Thomas Cartwright</a> (<a href="date.1535-1603.12.27">1535–December 27, 1603</a>): English Puritan leader; he taught and studied at the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25universityOfCambridge_Place">University of Cambridge</a>; supporter of Presbyterianism; criticized the religious practices of the Church of England; author of <em>Reply to an Answer made of Mr. Dr. Whitgift</em> (1573) which defended the Puritan manifesto <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25AdmonitionToParliament1572"><em>Admonition to Parliament</em></a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.WilliamPerkins">William Perkins</a> (<a href="date.1558-1602">1558–1602</a>): Anglican <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25theologian">theologian</a> and preacher; he studied at Cambridge; popularized <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ReformedGroup_Denom">Reformed</a> and practical theologies through his many writings.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.thomasgoodwin">Thomas Goodwin</a> (<a href="date.1600.10.5-1680.2.23">October 5, 1600–February 23, 1680</a>): English Puritan leader; <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Congregationalism">Congregationalist</a>; chaplain to Oliver Cromwell; member of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25WestminsterAssembly1643">Westminster Assembly</a>. </li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25JohnMilton_Person">John Milton</a> (<a href="date.1608.12.9-1674.11.8">December 9, 1608–November 8, 1674</a>): English Puritan <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%23poet">poet</a> and controversialist; author of <em>Paradise Lost</em> which describes a Puritan worldview of history and humanity; author of several political pamphlets which charged the church and state of England with corruption.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.RichardBaxter.1">Richard Baxter</a> (<a href="date.1615.11.12-1691.12.8">November 12, 1615–December 8, 1691</a>): English Puritan preacher and theologian; chaplain in Cromwell's army and later to <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25KingCharlesIIGreatBritain_Person">Charles II</a>; supporter of religious tolerance; author of many Puritan writings including <em>Reformed Liturgy</em> which is a Puritan alternative to the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25BookOfCommonPrayer_Writing">Book of Common Prayer</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25JohnOwen_Person">John Owen</a> (<a href="date.1616-1683.8.24">1616–August 24, 1683</a>): English Puritan pastor and theologian; vice-chancellor of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Oxford">Oxford University</a>; member of Parliament; author of several Reformed doctrinal works.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25PilgrimFathers_People">Pilgrim Fathers</a> (<a href="date.1620">1620</a>): English Separatists; founders of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%40PlymouthMassachusetts">Plymouth</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.JohnBunyan">John Bunyan</a> (<a href="date.1628.11.28-1688.8.31">November 28, 1628–August 31, 1688</a>): Puritan preacher; author of <em>The Pilgrim's Progress</em> which was written while he was imprisoned for holding an illegal worship service; he fought in the English Civil War.</li>
</ul>

<h2>Key Concepts</h2>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ActsofUniformity">Acts of Uniformity</a> were passed by Parliament to regulate the practice of Christianity in England. </li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Anglicanism_Belief">Anglicanism</a> combines elements of Protestantism and Catholicism.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Baptists">Baptists</a> were established by Separatists that left England and settled in Holland; affirmed believer's baptism.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ChurchOfEngland">Church of England</a> was the state church in England.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Congregationalism">Congregationalism</a> was the form of church government advocated for by Dissenters.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Dissenters">Dissenters</a> disagreed with the Church of England on church government.</li>
<li>Dissenting Brethren, Congregationalists who were a part of Westminster Assembly, advocated for tolerance of various forms of church government.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25HighChurchmen">High Churchmen</a> advocated for high place of church authority, the episcopate, and the sacraments. </li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25kingship">Kingship</a>: the divine right to rule as king.</li>
<li>Parliamentarians supported the English Parliament and opposed Charles I and the Royalists; also known as Roundheads.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Presbyterianism">Presbyterianism</a> was the form of church government advocated for by Puritans.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Puritanism_Belief">Puritanism</a> emphasized purification of the church and society.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=lco.Religious_denominations">Religious Denominations</a> were established by Separatists. </li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ReligiousToleration">Religious Toleration</a> was promoted by Dissenters.</li>
<li>Royalists supported Charles I and opposed the Parliamentarians; also known as Cavaliers.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25separatists">Separatists</a> left the Church of England and fled to the Netherlands and North America.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25SocietyOfFriends">Society of Friends</a> was founded by George Fox and Margaret Fell.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25WestminsterAssembly1643">Westminster Assembly</a> assembled to reform the Church of England.</li>
</ul>

<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25WestminsterConfession">Westminster Confession</a> (Westminster Assembly, <a href="date.1648">1648</a>): Presbyterian confession of faith. </li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25SavoyDeclaration_Writing">Savoy Declaration</a> (Thomas Goodwin and John Owen, <a href="date.1658">1658</a>): Congregational confession of faith; modified version of the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25WestminsterConfession">Westminster Confession</a>; urged toleration between Congregationalists and Presbyterians.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25LondonBaptistConfessionOfFaith">London Baptist Confession of Faith</a> (<a href="date.1689">1689</a>): Particular Baptist confession of faith.</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="bk.%law">Legislative Acts</a></h3>
<ul>
<li>Grand Remonstrance (<a href="date.1641">1641</a>): issued by Parliament; presented to Charles I; list of grievances against the king; gave Parliament the authority to approve the king's ministers and to set up an Assembly of Divines to supervise church reforms.</li>
<li>Clarendon Code (<a href="date.1661-1665">1661–1665</a>): four acts of Parliament intended to suppress <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Dissenters">Dissenters</a> within the Church of England; includes the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25CorporationAct1661">Corporation Act (1661)</a>, Act of Uniformity (1662), <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ConventiclesActs1664And1670">Conventicle Act (1664)</a>, and the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25FiveMileAct">Five Mile Act (1665)</a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25TolerationAct">Toleration Act 1688</a>: issued by Parliament; granted religious freedom to Dissenters.</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25literature">Literature</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ParadiseLost_Writing"><em>Paradise Lost</em></a> (John Milton, <a href="date.1667">1667</a>): epic poem which describes the Puritan worldview of history and humanity.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25PilgrimsProgress_Writing"><em>The Pilgrim's Progress</em></a> (John Bunyan, <a href="date.1678">1678</a>): allegory of the Christian life; written while Bunyan was imprisoned for holding illegal religious meetings.</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25liturgy">Liturgy</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25BookOfCommonPrayer_Writing">Book of Common Prayer</a> (Thomas Cranmer, <a href="date.1548">1548</a>): liturgical book of the Church of England; its use was required by Charles I for all churches.</li>
<li><em>Reformed Liturgy</em> (Richard Baxter, c. 1661): Puritan alternative to the Book of Common Prayer.</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="bk.%manifesto">Manifestos</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25AdmonitionToParliament1572">Admonition to Parliament</a> (John Field and Thomas Wilcox, <a href="date.1572">1572</a>): Puritan manifesto; addressed to English Parliament during the reign of Elizabeth I; argued against the episcopacy, urged for greater reliance on the Bible, and further purification of the Church of England. </li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="bk.%petition">Petitions</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25MillenaryPetition_Writing">Millenary Petition</a> (Puritans, <a href="date.1603">1603</a>): addressed to John I; identified reforms needed in the Church of England; prompted the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25HamptonCourtConference">Hampton Court Conference</a>. </li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="bk.%treaty">Treaties</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25MayflowerCompact_Writing">Mayflower Compact</a> (<a href="date.1620">1620</a>): governing document of Puritan settlement at Plymouth, Massachusetts.</li>
</ul>
