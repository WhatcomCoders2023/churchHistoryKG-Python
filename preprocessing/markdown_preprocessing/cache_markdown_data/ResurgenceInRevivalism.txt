<h1>A Resurgence in Revivalism and Evangelical Piety Reawakens Transatlantic Christianity</h1>
<ul>
<li><strong>Period:</strong> !!smallcaps|ad!! <a href="date.1787-1920">1787-1920</a></li>
<li><strong>Description:</strong> Revivalism spread through North America, Great Britain, and Europe, inspiring various renewal movements within evangelicalism and Pietism.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Summary</h2>
<p>The Second Great Awakening marked the beginning of modern revivalism, a movement characterized by multiday meetings and personal conversion. Protestantism spread rapidly in the United States as revivals erupted in the universities of New England before spreading west to the American frontier. Revivalism, likewise, emerged across the Atlantic as evangelicalism and Pietism were reawakened in Europe. This renewal of spiritual life led to the establishment of universities, colleges, and voluntary societies and a renewed interest in social reform and missions. Theologically, modern revivalism typically emphasized personal piety, often paired with emotional experiences, and an individual's free will to choose salvation.</p>
<h2>Key Developments</h2>
<!--- A bulleted list of important developments during that period.-->

<ul>
<li>Interest in <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25religion">religion</a> declined following the period of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25revolution">revolution</a> (<a href="date.1776-1787">1776-1787</a>).</li>
<li>Interest in religion renewed as the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.tle%3asecondgreatawakeningbegins">Second Great Awakening began</a> in New England (<a href="date.1787-1825">1787-1825</a>).</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25voluntaryAssociations">Voluntary</a> and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25mission">mission</a> societies were established to support social reform and foreign missions (<a href="date.1787-1920">1787–1920</a>).</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Revivalism_Belief">Revivalism</a> faced criticism from those opposed to overt displays of emotion in worship (<a href="date.1787-1920">1787–1920</a>).</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Methodism_Belief">Methodism</a> and, to a lesser degree, <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Baptists">Baptists</a> increased in the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%40UnitedStates">United States</a> (<a href="date.1700+100+l-1800+100+l">late 1700s–late 1800s</a>.</li>
<li>A renewal of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Pietism_Belief">Pietism</a> sparked revival and reform in <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%40Europe">Europe</a> (<a href="date.1700+100+l-1800+100+e">late 1700s–early 1800s</a>).</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.pt%3aRevival">Revival</a> leaders in <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%40NorthAmerica">North America</a> turned their attention to the <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25frontier">frontier</a> as pioneers moved westward (<a href="date.1800+100">1800s</a>).</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25campMeeting">Camp meetings</a>, multiday gatherings held outdoors that emphasized personal conversion, became an essential method of evangelism and revival (<a href="date.1800+100">1800s</a>).</li>
<li>Colleges and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25seminary">seminaries</a> were founded (<a href="date.1800+100">1800s</a>).</li>
<li>New religious movements and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Denominations">denominations</a> were established as <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Protestantism_Belief">Protestantism</a> diversified during the era of revivalism (<a href="date.1800+100-1900+100+e">1800s–early 1900s</a>).</li>
<li>Women stepped into more visible <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25womenInChurchLeadership">leadership roles</a> within the church (<a href="date.1800+100-1900+100">1800s–1900s</a>).</li>
<li>Revivals later spread to urban areas as <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Evangelicalism_Belief">evangelicalism</a> was revived in cities (<a href="date.1830+10">1830s</a>).</li>
<li>Religious revival spread across <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25GreatBritain_Place">Great Britain</a> (<a href="date.1859">1859</a>).</li>
</ul>

<h3><a href="bk.%journal">Journals</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="bk.%JournalFrancisAsbury_Writing"><em>Journal of Rev. Francis Asbury:
Bishop of the Methodist Episcopal Church</em></a> (<a href="date.1821">1821</a>):  describes Asbury's life as a circuit rider and the revival of religion in the United States; mentions a large number of frontier camp meetings.[^2]</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="bk.%lecture">Lectures</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="bk.%LecturesRevivalsReligion_Writing"><em>Lectures on Revivals of Religion</em></a> (Charles G. Finney, <a href="date.1835">1835</a>: addresses revivalism and emphasizes humanity's ability to believe, as opposed to Calvinism's emphasis on conversion as a work of the Holy Spirit; this work popularized Finney's methods of evangelism and revivalism.</li>
</ul>
<h3><a href="bk.%treatise">Treatises</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="bk.%PleaForTheWest_Writing"><em>A Plea for the West</em></a> (Lyman Beecher, <a href="date.1835">1835</a>): advocates for the western spread of a Protestant evangelicalism; includes Beecher's anti-Catholic views and opposition to the spread of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25RomanCatholicism_Belief">Roman Catholicism</a> in the United States.</li>
</ul>
