<h1>Christian Apologists Defend the New Faith</h1>
<ul>
<li><strong>Period:</strong> !!smallcaps|ad!! 150-381</li>
<li><strong>Description:</strong> Early Christians began writing apologetic works as well as interacting with philosophy and other worldviews, including Judaism, to defend and define the faith.</li>
</ul>
<h2>Key Developments</h2>
<!--- A bulleted list of important developments during that period.-->

<ul>
<li>Christianity spread across the Roman empire.</li>
<li>Christianity separated from Judaism and became its own religion.</li>
<li>Christianity faced attacks from pagans intellectuals.</li>
<li>Christian apologists defend the faith against arguments from Judaism and paganism.</li>
<li>Christian thinkers engaged other worldviews and philosophies to defend the new faith; some held a positive view of philosophy while others argued it was at odds with Christianity.</li>
<li>Catechical and theological schools were established in various locations.</li>
<li>Paganism declines and Christianity becomes the religion of the Roman Empire.</li>
</ul>

<h3><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Apologists">Apologists</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Quadratus_Person">Quadratus</a> (c. 2nd century AD): earliest known Christian apologist; author of <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25ApologyOfQuadratus_Writing"><em>Apology of Quadratus</em></a> which is one of the earliest known Christian apologies.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.JustinMartyr">Justin Martyr</a> (AD 100–165): Christian apologist; started schools in <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Ephesus_Place">Ephesus</a> and Rome; author of <em>First Apology</em>, <em>Second Apology</em>, and <a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25DialogueWithTrypho_Writing"><em>Dialogue with Trypho</em></a>.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.ClementAlexandria">Clement of Alexandria</a> (150–215): Greek theologian and philosopher; Latin Church Father; leader of School of Alexandria.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25Tertullian_Person">Tertullian</a> (c. AD 160–230): African Church Father and apologist; author of several works that offer a defense of the Christian faith including <em>Apoligeticum</em> and <em>An Answer to the Jews</em>; he is influenced by Stoicism.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bio.Origen">Origen</a> (185–254): Egyptian theologian; Greek Church Father; leader of School of Alexandria.</li>
</ul>

<h3><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25apology.defense">Apologies</a></h3>
<ul>
<li><em>First Apology</em> (AD 150): written by Justin Martyr; apologetic defense of the Christian faith addressed to the emperor.</li>
<li><a href="https://ref.ly/logos4/Factbook?ref=bk.%25DialogueWithTrypho_Writing"><em>Dialogue with Trypho</em></a> (Justin Martyr, c. 155–160): </li>
<li><em>An Answer to the Jews</em> (Tertullian, c. 198): asserts Christ as the fulfillment in response to objections from Judaism.</li>
<li><em>Exhortation to the Greeks</em> (Clement of Alexandria, late 2nd century): egnages Scripture and philosophy; urges pagans to convert to Christianity.</li>
</ul>
